Data Platform Migration — Aligning 4 VPs on a $3M Infrastructure Decision

Building alignment for migrating from a legacy data warehouse to a modern lakehouse architecture when Engineering is supportive, Finance is skeptical, Product is neutral, and the CDO has been burned by the last migration.

Skill: stakeholder-alignment Date: March 2026 Source: PM Skills Arsenal

Executive Summary

A $3M migration from Redshift to Databricks lakehouse requires alignment from four VPs at a Series D SaaS company ($120M ARR). The current alignment landscape: VP Engineering is a champion who wants modern tooling and will spend political capital, VP Finance is a skeptic burned by last year's Snowflake migration that went $1.2M over budget, VP Product is neutral but cares about data availability for product analytics, and the CDO is cautious because she led the failed Snowflake migration and is politically exposed H direct observation + secondhand reports. The biggest risk is not the Finance skeptic — it is the CDO, whose caution is rational and whose political exposure means she needs this migration to succeed more than anyone but cannot afford to champion it publicly until she has cover H incentive analysis + decision archaeology. The correct alignment sequence is: (1) lock in VP Engineering's active sponsorship, (2) convert VP Product with data availability guarantees, (3) use Engineering + Product coalition to give the CDO political cover, (4) approach VP Finance last with a phased budget proposal that explicitly addresses the Snowflake precedent. Recommended first move: Schedule a 1:1 with VP Engineering this week to formalize her championship and co-design the phased migration plan she can present to the CDO.

Decision Archaeology: The Snowflake Shadow

Why stakeholders hold their current positions — uncovering the history, incentives, and trust dynamics that shape today's alignment landscape. This section is load-bearing: you cannot align these stakeholders without understanding what happened 14 months ago.

3a. The Snowflake Migration Story

14 months ago, the company attempted to migrate from Redshift to Snowflake. Maria Torres (CDO) led the initiative. The original budget was $1.8M over 12 months. It went $1.2M over budget ($3M total), took 18 months instead of 12, caused 3 weeks of partial data outage during the cutover, and was ultimately abandoned at 60% completion when the board questioned the ROI. Maria's quarterly performance review cited "poor vendor management and underscoped migration planning." T2 secondhand from Maria's direct report + Finance post-mortem document

What actually went wrong: The Snowflake migration failed for three specific reasons — (1) big-bang cutover strategy with no parallel-run period, (2) the vendor's SOW underestimated schema complexity by 3x (400 tables with cross-dependencies vs. 150 estimated), and (3) no phase gates — the team committed the full $3M before validating that the first 50-table migration worked. These were scoping and project management failures, not technology failures. T2 post-mortem document + engineering retrospective

Maria's political exposure: Maria was the public face of the Snowflake migration. When it failed, she bore the blame even though the scoping was done by an external consulting firm. Her credibility with the board is damaged. She cannot afford another infrastructure failure. But here is the paradox: she also cannot afford to keep Redshift, because the 3x query time degradation is now visible to the CEO, and the data team spending 40% of time on maintenance is a metric the board tracks. Maria needs to move, but she needs cover. T3 incentive analysis + T2 secondhand report from CDO's chief of staff

Alignment Sequence: The Domino Logic

The order matters. Each conversation changes the next stakeholder's calculus. Approaching a blocker before building coalition support is the most common alignment failure.

4a. Sequencing Rules Applied

RuleApplication
Start with strongest championSarah Chen — formalize her championship and co-design the phased plan
Then swing voters (before blockers frame)Priya Sharma — convert with data availability guarantees before anyone frames the migration as "risky for product"
Then cautious decision ownerMaria Torres — approach with Engineering + Product coalition backing and a plan that addresses every Snowflake failure mode
Approach budget authority lastDavid Okafor — with 3-VP technical consensus, his skepticism becomes isolated and the phase-gated budget addresses his specific concern

Sarah Chen (VP Engineering) — CHAMPION → CHAMPION (activate)

Action: 1:1 meeting (60 min). Co-design the phased migration plan. Ask Sarah to present the technical architecture at the next leadership meeting. Frame it as her initiative, not yours — her credibility is higher than yours for this decision. T1

Timing: Week 1

Dependency: None — this is the first domino

Success criteria: Sarah agrees to co-present the migration plan. She assigns a senior architect to draft the phased technical proposal.

What this unlocks: Sarah's visible sponsorship gives Maria Torres political cover and gives Priya Sharma technical confidence.

3

Maria Torres (CDO) — SKEPTIC → SUPPORTER → eventual CHAMPION

Action: 1:1 meeting (45 min). This is the most delicate conversation. Do NOT open with "we should migrate to Databricks." Open with: "Maria, I know the Redshift performance issues are visible to the CEO, and I know you're thinking about options. Sarah and Priya are both interested in a phased approach that addresses every issue from the Snowflake experience. I'd like to walk through the plan — not to put you on the spot, but to see if it meets your bar." T3 framing designed to respect her political exposure

Key framing for Maria:

  • Address the Snowflake ghost directly: "This plan is designed around the three specific things that went wrong with Snowflake — phased instead of big-bang, internal-led instead of vendor-led, phase gates with kill switches."
  • Give her credit, not blame: "You're the person who understands what went wrong better than anyone. That experience makes you the right decision owner for this — you won't let the same mistakes happen."
  • Show the coalition cover: "Sarah is already sponsoring this technically, and Priya's team has confirmed zero-downtime requirements are met by the parallel-run approach."
  • Make it her decision to champion: "If you're comfortable with the plan, I think you presenting this to the CEO with Sarah would show the board that the data org learned from Snowflake and is executing rigorously."

Timing: Week 3 (after Engineering + Product are aligned)

Dependency: Steps 1 and 2 complete

Success criteria: Maria agrees to review the detailed technical plan. She schedules a follow-up with Sarah's senior architect. She does NOT need to verbally champion it yet — agreement to engage is the milestone.

What this unlocks: With CDO engaged (even cautiously), the Finance conversation becomes "the technical leaders have consensus and the CDO is on board — we need your budget framework."

↓
4

David Okafor (VP Finance) — SKEPTIC → SUPPORTER

Action: Formal budget review meeting (60 min). Sarah presents the phased budget (not you, not Maria — Sarah has no Snowflake baggage with David). The proposal structure: Phase 1 ($400K, 3 months) migrates the 50 highest-impact tables + validates the architecture. Go/no-go gate. Phase 2 ($1.1M, 6 months) migrates remaining tables + builds new lakehouse features. Go/no-go gate. Phase 3 ($1.5M, 9 months) decommissions Redshift + optimizes. Kill switch at each gate. T3

Key framing for David:

  • Acknowledge the Snowflake precedent directly: "David, we know the Snowflake experience is relevant here. This proposal is structured to prevent every specific failure mode from that project."
  • Phase gates = budget control: "You're not approving $3M — you're approving $400K with the option to continue or stop at each gate. Maximum exposure at any point is the current phase's budget."
  • Cost of inaction: "Redshift maintenance costs $480K/year in engineering time (40% of data team on maintenance). Doing nothing is not $0 — it's $480K/year and accelerating."
  • Show consensus: "Engineering, Product, and the CDO have all reviewed this plan. You're the last piece of the puzzle, not the first person being asked."

Timing: Week 4-5 (after CDO engagement confirmed)

Dependency: Steps 1, 2, and 3 complete

Success criteria: David approves Phase 1 budget ($400K) with explicit go/no-go criteria for Phase 2. Full $3M approval is not needed — phased approval is the design.

Objection Pre-emption Playbook

Anticipated objections, steel-manned to be stronger than the stakeholder would state them, with prepared responses. These are not straw men — these are the genuine concerns of intelligent people protecting their organizations.

Objection 1: "We already burned $3M on Snowflake. Why would Databricks be different?"

Stakeholder(s): David Okafor (Finance), Maria Torres (CDO)
Objection type: Rational + Emotional
Underlying concern: David fears another budget overrun that damages his credibility with the board. Maria fears another failure that ends her career at this company. Both are anchoring on the most available comparison: the Snowflake disaster. T1 T2

◈

Steel-manned version: "The Snowflake migration was led by the same company, approved by largely the same leadership team, and followed the same general pattern: big budget, new technology, promised improvements. It failed catastrophically. What structural evidence exists that this time will be different? The technology changed (Databricks vs. Snowflake), but the organization, decision-making process, and budget governance are identical. Changing the technology without changing the execution approach is the definition of doing the same thing and expecting different results."

Response:

  • Acknowledge the pattern directly: "You're right that the technology change alone doesn't justify confidence. What's different is the execution structure — this plan was designed specifically around the three root causes of the Snowflake failure."
  • Root cause 1 — Big-bang cutover: "Snowflake was all-or-nothing. This plan runs Databricks in parallel with Redshift for the entire migration. We don't decommission Redshift until 100% of workloads are validated on Databricks. Zero-downtime migration." T2
  • Root cause 2 — Vendor-led execution: "Snowflake was led by an external consulting firm that underestimated schema complexity by 3x. This migration is led by our data engineering team with Databricks professional services in a support role, not lead role." T2
  • Root cause 3 — No phase gates: "Snowflake committed the full budget before validating anything. This plan has go/no-go gates at $400K, $1.5M, and $3M. At each gate, we either continue, adjust, or kill. Maximum exposure at any point is the current phase's budget."

Evidence required: Snowflake post-mortem document (already available), phased migration plan with explicit kill criteria T2

Escalation path: If David remains unconvinced after seeing the phase-gated structure, offer a smaller Phase 0 ($150K, 6 weeks) proof-of-concept with 10 tables. Risk: timeline extends by 6 weeks but budget exposure drops to $150K.

Objection 2: "The data team spends 40% on maintenance today. Migration will make that 80% for 18 months."

Stakeholder(s): Priya Sharma (Product), Maria Torres (CDO)
Objection type: Rational
Underlying concern: Priya worries that the data team will be so consumed by migration that product analytics support degrades. Maria worries that data team capacity constraints will cause the migration to drag, creating another Snowflake timeline failure. T2

◈

Steel-manned version: "Your data team is already at capacity — 40% on maintenance, 60% on actual data work. A migration adds a massive project on top of that. Either you hire expensive contractors (adding to the $3M budget), or you pull the data team off product support (degrading data availability for product analytics, which is exactly what the migration is supposed to fix). The migration creates a 12-18 month valley where data service quality gets worse before it gets better. Can the business afford that valley?"

Response:

  • The maintenance burden is the argument FOR migration, not against it: "40% on maintenance is $480K/year in opportunity cost. That number is growing — 12 months ago it was 25%. At current trajectory, it will be 55% in 12 months. The valley you're describing is already here; it's just getting deeper."
  • Phased migration reduces the capacity hit: "Phase 1 migrates only the 50 highest-impact tables (the ones causing the most maintenance). After Phase 1, the maintenance burden drops to ~25% because the most problematic tables are on the new platform. Phase 2 and 3 are net-positive for capacity." T3
  • Dedicated migration squad: "The plan includes a 3-person dedicated migration squad (2 internal + 1 Databricks specialist). Product analytics support is maintained by the existing data team with no reduction in headcount. The migration is additive capacity, not reallocated capacity." T3

Evidence required: Current maintenance time breakdown by table/workload (to validate the "50 highest-impact tables" claim), capacity plan showing migration squad doesn't reduce product analytics support T2

Escalation path: If capacity concerns persist, offer to hire 2 contract data engineers specifically for migration, funded from Phase 1 budget. Increases Phase 1 cost by $100K but eliminates the capacity objection.

Objection 3: "Why Databricks? Why not just optimize Redshift?"

Stakeholder(s): David Okafor (Finance), potentially Maria Torres (CDO)
Objection type: Rational
Underlying concern: David sees a $3M migration vs. a cheaper optimization path. Maria may prefer optimization because it's lower-risk politically — "we improved what we have" is safer than "we migrated to something new." T3

◈

Steel-manned version: "Redshift has gotten dramatically better in the last 2 years — Redshift Serverless, RA3 instances, AQUA acceleration. You haven't even tried the latest features. A $300K Redshift optimization project could address query performance without any migration risk, without any team disruption, and without triggering Snowflake flashbacks across the leadership team. Why spend 10x more on migration when optimization might solve the immediate problem?"

Response:

  • We've already optimized Redshift twice: "The data team ran two optimization sprints in the last 12 months — migrated to RA3 instances (cost: $120K) and restructured the 20 largest tables (cost: 6 engineering-weeks). Query times improved 30% temporarily, then degraded again within 4 months as data volume grew. We've hit the ceiling of what Redshift optimization can deliver given our growth rate." T2
  • The problem is architectural, not configurational: "Redshift is a columnar data warehouse. Our needs have grown beyond warehousing — we need real-time streaming for product analytics, native ML infrastructure for Priya's team, and unified batch + streaming pipelines. Redshift doesn't do these things, regardless of optimization. Databricks lakehouse is architecturally designed for this workload." T2
  • Total cost comparison: "Redshift optimization: $300K now, repeated every 6-12 months ($300K/year), with maintenance burden still at 40%+ ($480K/year). Total 3-year cost: $2.3M+ with degrading performance. Databricks migration: $3M over 18 months, maintenance burden drops to 15% ($180K/year), no repeated optimization cycles. Total 3-year cost: $3.5M with improving performance. The delta is $1.2M for a fundamentally better platform." T3

Evidence required: Results from previous Redshift optimization sprints (available from data team), Redshift vs. Databricks TCO model for 3-year horizon T2

Escalation path: If David insists on optimization first, offer a compromise: "Run a 4-week Redshift optimization sprint ($50K). If query times improve by 50%+ and maintenance burden drops below 30%, we pause the migration discussion. If they don't, we proceed with Phase 1." This is a low-cost experiment that likely validates the migration need.

Objection 4: "I don't trust that the phase gates are real. Last time we had 'checkpoints' too."

Stakeholder(s): David Okafor (Finance), Maria Torres (CDO)
Objection type: Emotional + Structural
Underlying concern: The Snowflake migration technically had "milestones" in the original plan, but they were soft checkpoints with no kill criteria. When the project went over budget, there was no mechanism to stop it — sunk cost fallacy took over. David and Maria both know that "phase gates" can be performative. T2

◈

Steel-manned version: "Phase gates are a project management cliche. Every failed project had 'phase gates' in the plan. The problem is that nobody exercises the kill switch because by the time a gate arrives, the team is emotionally invested, the sunk cost is large, and the political cost of admitting failure is higher than the cost of continuing. What makes your phase gates different from the ones we ignored during Snowflake?"

Response:

  • The kill criteria are quantitative, not qualitative: "Phase 1 gate criteria: (1) 50 tables migrated with <5% query performance regression vs. Redshift, (2) parallel-run validated with zero data discrepancies for 2 consecutive weeks, (3) total Phase 1 spend within $400K +/- 10%. If any criterion fails, Phase 2 does not start. These are measurable, binary outcomes — not 'are we comfortable proceeding?'"
  • The decision-maker at each gate is Finance, not the project team: "David, you personally approve each phase transition. The project team presents results against the pre-agreed criteria. If the criteria aren't met, you have explicit authority to pause or kill. This is your kill switch, not ours."
  • Budget is released per-phase, not upfront: "Unlike Snowflake where $3M was committed upfront, each phase requires a separate budget approval. Phase 2 funds are not allocated until Phase 1 gate is passed. There is no sunk cost trap because the money for future phases doesn't exist yet."

Evidence required: Written phase gate criteria document with Finance sign-off, explicit budget release mechanism in the capital approval process T3

Escalation path: Offer to add an independent technical review at each phase gate — an external advisor (not the migration vendor) validates whether gate criteria are met. Cost: ~$15K per review. Worth it for credibility.

Adversarial Self-Critique

Weakness 1: This Strategy Assumes Maria Can Be Converted — What If She's Right To Block?

What I assumed: Maria Torres is cautious but convertible — she needs political cover, not persuasion. The alignment strategy is designed to surround her with coalition support until she's comfortable championing the migration.

What could be wrong: Maria may have information I don't — a private conversation with the CEO indicating that infrastructure spend is frozen, a board directive to reduce data team headcount, or a personal decision to leave the company in the next 6 months. If any of these are true, her caution is not political exposure from Snowflake; it's rational self-preservation based on information this strategy doesn't account for. Approaching her with coalition pressure when she has private reasons to block could damage the relationship and waste the coalition's political capital.

Evidence that would disprove this analysis: Maria starts declining all infrastructure-related meetings (not just this one). She begins restructuring her team in ways that suggest downsizing rather than growth. The CEO stops mentioning data platform improvements in leadership meetings.

Invalidation trigger: If Maria delegates the migration decision to her director-level report with no deadline for a decision → she's not cautious, she's blocking through bureaucracy. Re-classify her as BLOCKER and shift to escalation strategy.

Weakness 2: The Phase-Gated Approach May Create Its Own Failure Mode

What I assumed: Phase gates with kill switches address the Finance and CDO objections and create genuine accountability. This is the centerpiece of the budget proposal.

What could be wrong: Phase gates create three risks the strategy doesn't address: (1) Phase 1 succeeds but Phase 2 is blocked due to unrelated budget constraints — you've migrated 50 tables to Databricks but 350 remain on Redshift, creating a hybrid nightmare that's worse than either system alone. (2) The kill switch creates a "pause" incentive — at each gate, risk-averse stakeholders have a natural opportunity to say "let's pause and reassess," which in practice means "let's kill this slowly." (3) Phase 1 scope is too small to prove the architecture — 50 tables may not stress-test the complex cross-table dependencies that killed Snowflake.

Evidence that would disprove this analysis: Phase 1 scope is validated by Sarah's architect as genuinely representative of the migration's complexity (not just the easy tables). The budget approval for Phases 2-3 is pre-committed contingent on Phase 1 criteria being met (not re-evaluated from scratch).

Invalidation trigger: If Phase 1 completes successfully but Phase 2 approval takes more than 4 weeks → the phase-gated approach is being weaponized as a delay mechanism. Escalate to the CEO with Phase 1 results.

Weakness 3: This Strategy Over-Indexes on the Snowflake Narrative and Under-Indexes on the Databricks Technical Case

What I assumed: The alignment challenge is primarily political (Snowflake trauma, CDO exposure, Finance skepticism), and the technical case for Databricks is sound.

What could be wrong: I've spent 80% of this strategy on political alignment and 20% on technical merit. If the Databricks technical case has genuine weaknesses — higher-than-expected costs, poor integration with the company's existing BI stack, steep learning curve for the data team — the political alignment will succeed but the migration will fail operationally. That would be worse than the Snowflake failure because this time the alignment was "successful" but the outcome was the same.

Evidence that would disprove this analysis: An independent technical assessment by an engineer who has no stake in the decision confirms Databricks is the right architecture. The data team (not leadership) validates that they can execute the migration with the proposed resources.

Invalidation trigger: If Sarah's senior architect raises concerns about Databricks' suitability during the technical proposal drafting → pause the alignment campaign and resolve the technical question first. A politically aligned initiative that fails technically is career-ending for everyone involved.

Try It Yourself: Quick-Start Guide

Apply this framework to your own stakeholder alignment challenge in 4 steps:

  1. Map your stakeholders (30 min)
    List every person who can approve, block, or influence the decision. Score each on Power (H/M/L), Interest (H/M/L), and Position (Champion/Supporter/Neutral/Skeptic/Blocker). Tag every assessment with an evidence tier — if you're guessing, mark it T5-T6 and plan to validate.
  1. Run Decision Archaeology (45 min)
    For every stakeholder who isn't a champion, ask: "What happened in the past that shapes their current position?" Failed projects, budget overruns, organizational changes, personal career events. The current decision doesn't exist in a vacuum — every position has a history. Find it.
  1. Design your alignment sequence (30 min)
    Start with your strongest champion. Then convert swing voters. Then cautious decision-makers (with coalition backing). Approach blockers last, with maximum support. Never approach a blocker before you've built a coalition — isolated arguments lose to institutional inertia.
  1. Steel-man the objections (30 min)
    For each skeptic or blocker, write their strongest possible objection — stronger than they would articulate it themselves. Then prepare your response. If you can't respond to the steel-manned version, you haven't done enough homework.

Output: You'll have a stakeholder alignment strategy ready to execute in ~2.5 hours. The quality improves dramatically when you use the full stakeholder-alignment skill with Claude Code.
