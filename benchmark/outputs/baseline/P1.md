# P1 — Baseline (Condition A)

## Competitive Landscape Overview: Project Management Tools for Marketing Teams

This overview covers Asana, Monday.com, and Notion as your primary named competitors, with context on the broader landscape, where each player is strong, where they fall short for marketing teams specifically, and what that means for your positioning and roadmap.

---

### The Context You're Operating In

The project management software market is crowded, but "crowded" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. The reality is that most project management tools were built for engineering or generic knowledge work, and marketing teams have historically been underserved. They use Asana because it was adopted company-wide, or Monday.com because a project manager set it up, or Notion because someone in a blog post called it a "second brain." Very few tools were designed from the ground up for how marketing actually works: campaigns with deadlines tied to external events, asset-heavy workflows, cross-functional dependencies on design and legal and brand, and a constant need to report results upward.

That gap is your entry point. Here's how the incumbents are positioned:

---

### Asana

**Who it's for (in practice):** Mid-to-large teams, especially companies that have already standardized on it for engineering or operations. Asana has become the "safe choice" — it's in more G2 comparisons, more enterprise procurement lists, and more IT-approved vendor catalogs than almost any other tool in the space.

**Where it's strong:**
- Task and dependency management is genuinely excellent. Timelines, Gantt-style views, and cross-project dependencies are mature features.
- Workflow automation (Rules) allows teams to create simple if-then automations without code.
- Enterprise security and compliance features are well-developed — SSO, audit logs, data residency options.
- Integrations are broad: Slack, Salesforce, Adobe Creative Cloud, and most of the standard marketing stack.
- Has a templates library with marketing-specific workflows (campaign tracking, editorial calendar, event planning).

**Where it falls short for marketing teams:**
- Built primarily around tasks, not the artifacts and content that marketing produces. There's no native understanding of a "creative brief" or a "campaign."
- Reporting is functional but not marketing-oriented. You can pull task completion rates; you can't easily see "how did this campaign perform relative to plan?"
- The learning curve is real. Marketing teams — unlike engineering teams — often have mixed technical comfort levels. Asana's power comes with complexity.
- Pricing is steep for larger teams. The Business tier ($24.99/user/month as of 2024) adds up fast, and many marketing features sit behind that paywall.
- The UI, while improved, still feels like a productivity tool dressed up for enterprise, not a purpose-built marketing workspace.

**Their strategic bet:** Asana is investing heavily in AI (Asana Intelligence), adding smart summaries, goal tracking assistance, and workload recommendations. They are moving up-market toward enterprise, which means SMB and mid-market marketing teams are becoming less of a focus.

---

### Monday.com

**Who it's for (in practice):** Operations-oriented teams and non-technical users who want flexibility without code. Monday has positioned itself as the "Work OS" — a platform on which you build your own workflows, not a point solution.

**Where it's strong:**
- Visual flexibility is genuinely differentiating. Monday lets teams customize boards, views, and data columns in ways that feel intuitive to non-developers.
- Monday Marketing (their vertical product) is a real attempt to speak to marketing buyers — it has campaign management, creative briefs, and content calendars built in.
- Their platform play (Monday Apps, Monday Workflows) means there is a growing ecosystem of third-party extensions.
- Sales motion is strong — they are good at enterprise deals and have invested heavily in customer success, which reduces churn for larger accounts.
- They've built dashboard views that marketing leaders can use for reporting without much setup.

**Where it falls short:**
- The "build your own Work OS" positioning is also a liability. Marketing teams often don't want to architect their own workflow tool — they want something that works out of the box for campaigns.
- Monday has many features, but depth suffers in places. Task dependencies and critical path analysis are less sophisticated than Asana's.
- The platform can feel disjointed. Marketing, Dev, Sales, and CRM products are sold as a suite but the integration between them is uneven.
- Pricing is complex and becomes expensive at scale. Their minimum seat count rules and tier thresholds frustrate small teams.
- Despite the "Monday Marketing" vertical, most of their case studies and brand identity still center on operations and project management, not marketing outcomes.

**Their strategic bet:** Monday is doubling down on the Work OS platform and trying to own more of the enterprise stack (CRM, service, dev). This means their marketing vertical is one of many bets, not their core identity. They will not win on marketing-specific depth.

---

### Notion

**Who it's for (in practice):** Knowledge workers and teams that value writing, documentation, and connected thinking. Notion started as a note-taking and wiki tool and has grown into a workspace that many teams use for project tracking, wikis, and light CRM — often not because it was the best choice for those jobs, but because they were already in Notion.

**Where it's strong:**
- The best-in-class writing and documentation experience. If marketing teams produce a lot of content — briefs, strategy docs, copy, brand guidelines — Notion is genuinely excellent for that.
- Flexibility. Notion databases, linked views, and relations mean a technical user can build remarkably sophisticated systems.
- Notion AI is well-integrated and genuinely useful for content drafting, summarizing, and filling templates.
- Brand and community. Notion has a cult-like following and an enormous template ecosystem. For indie brands and startups, being "a Notion team" is almost an identity.
- Competitive pricing relative to the others — particularly for smaller teams.

**Where it falls short for marketing teams:**
- Not a real project management tool. There is no true task dependency system, no critical path, no time tracking, no workload management. These are not edge cases — they are the core of running campaigns.
- Collaboration is awkward at scale. Notion pages can become messy as teams grow; governance is minimal.
- Notifications and task management are weak by design. Notion is not a place where you reliably know what's assigned to you or what's due.
- Performance degrades with large databases and complex linked views. This becomes a real pain point as teams scale.
- Notion is built around pages, not workflows. Marketing teams who need structured processes (campaign intake, asset approval, go/no-go gates) will fight the tool constantly.

**Their strategic bet:** Notion is building Notion AI and Notion Calendar into a unified "connected workspace" play. They are also expanding into team wikis and knowledge management more deliberately. Their bet is on "the single place where you write, think, and plan" — not on structured project delivery.

### The Competitive Landscape Map

A quick 2x2 framing that's useful for positioning:

**Axis 1: Generic vs. Marketing-Specific**
- Asana and Notion: Horizontal, general-purpose tools adapted for marketing
- Monday.com: Semi-vertical with a marketing product, but fundamentally a platform
- You: Opportunity to be the genuinely vertical, marketing-native tool

**Axis 2: Structured Workflows vs. Flexible Workspace**
- Asana: High structure, task-centric
- Monday.com: Medium structure, flexible boards
- Notion: Low structure, document-centric
- You: Opportunity to nail structured campaign workflows without the rigidity of Asana

The open space in the map: **Structured enough to run real campaigns, flexible enough for creative work, and built around marketing jobs-to-be-done — not adapted from engineering PM.**

---

### What Marketing Teams Actually Need (That None of These Nail)

Based on how marketing teams work, here are the jobs-to-be-done that are underserved across all three competitors:

1. **Campaign lifecycle management end-to-end** — from brief to launch to reporting, in one place. Right now teams stitch together Asana for tasks, Google Drive for assets, and a spreadsheet for reporting. No one has nailed the full loop.

2. **Asset and content workflow** — routing creative briefs, getting approvals, linking assets to tasks, tracking versions. Tools like Bynder and Wrike exist at the enterprise end; there's room in the mid-market.

3. **Marketing-specific reporting tied to execution** — not just "did we complete the tasks?" but "did we ship the campaign on time, and how did it perform relative to plan?" This is the gap between project management and marketing intelligence.

4. **Calendar-centric views** — marketing lives and dies on a content calendar and campaign calendar. Most PM tools treat calendar as a view of tasks, not as a first-class planning surface.

5. **Cross-functional visibility without giving everyone full access** — marketing teams often need to share status with leadership, agencies, and sales without inviting everyone into the full workspace. Stakeholder view management is weak across all three competitors.

### Positioning Implications for Your Roadmap

Given this landscape, here are the strategic implications:

**On positioning:**
- Don't compete as "another Asana" or "a better Monday." Compete as the first PM tool built for how marketing teams actually work.
- The framing that works: "Built for campaigns, not just tasks." Or more specifically: "From brief to launch to report — the only tool that understands a campaign."
- Your differentiation message should speak to the pain, not the features: "You already know how to manage marketing. You shouldn't have to teach your project management tool what a campaign is."

**On target customer:**
- The best initial beachhead is likely a marketing team of 5-25 people, either at a growth-stage startup or inside a mid-market company where the team has autonomy over their tools. Enterprise marketing teams are locked into IT-approved stacks (usually Asana or Jira). Freelancers and solopreneurs go to Notion.
- Content marketing teams and demand gen teams are your highest-probability early adopters. They have the most acute pain around campaign workflows and reporting.

**On roadmap:**
- Nail the campaign lifecycle first — brief, tasks, assets, approval, launch checklist. Don't try to be a wiki or a work OS.
- Integrate early with the tools marketing teams actually use: HubSpot, Salesforce, Google Analytics/GA4, Canva, Google Ads, Meta Ads Manager. Not just Slack and email like everyone else.
- Build reporting that connects execution to outcomes. Even a simple "we planned to launch 5 campaigns this quarter, launched 4, here's the timeline slip" report is more valuable to a marketing VP than any Gantt chart.
- Resist the temptation to build everything. Notion wins on flexibility; Asana wins on depth of task management. You win on being purpose-built. Every feature you add that isn't marketing-specific dilutes that story.

**On GTM:**
- Asana and Monday have significant brand awareness and sales muscle. You cannot out-spend them on paid acquisition.
- Your edge is in community, content, and word of mouth. Marketing teams talk to each other. A template library, a blog with genuinely useful campaign frameworks, and early case studies from recognizable marketing teams will carry you farther than outbound sales at this stage.
- Review sites (G2, Capterra) are where most marketing tool evaluations begin. Getting 50 strong reviews in the "marketing project management" category early is a real competitive moat.

### The Competitors You're Not Watching (But Should Be)

A few players that don't get mentioned but are relevant:

- **Wrike** — positioned as a marketing PM tool for enterprise. They have creative review features (proofing) and campaign management. Not well-known, but they compete directly in the marketing-specific PM space.
- **Teamwork** — strong with agencies, decent creative workflow features. Underrated in the agency and in-house creative team segment.
- **Airtable** — highly flexible database tool that many marketing teams use as a campaign tracker. It doesn't market itself as project management but it often competes in the same evaluation. The new Airtable with Interface Designer is a real step up.
- **ClickUp** — the everything-tool that's aggressively priced. Many teams trial ClickUp as a cheaper Asana; it loses on UX but gains on features. They have a marketing template set and they compete on price.
- **Loom + Figma + Slack combined** — the "cobbled together stack" is your real competition in many SMB evaluations. Teams who aren't ready to pay for a dedicated tool will keep duct-taping their workflow.

---

### Summary: What to Do With This

The landscape gives you a clear opportunity:

1. Asana owns enterprise task management but is not marketing-native and is moving further up-market.
2. Monday.com has a marketing product but is fundamentally a platform play — marketing is a vertical they serve, not the problem they were built to solve.
3. Notion is a writing tool that gets used for PM by accident — its limitations in structured workflow are real and frustrating for marketing teams at scale.

Your job is to build the tool that a VP of Marketing at a 100-person company would choose first — not because it integrates with Jira, but because it understands that a campaign has a brief, a budget, assets, approvals, a launch, and a retrospective. That workflow is not represented by any of the three tools you named.

Own that workflow, and you own a defensible position that the incumbents cannot easily copy without abandoning what makes them successful.
